Top 3 Leading Theories in Education Field

Behaviorism

Ivan Pavlov, John Watson, Burrhus F. Skinner, and Edward Thorndike.

Behaviorism was the first theory to impact the educational field in the early 1960s (Settler 1990). It seems to be the most prevalent learning theory in the U.S. education system. Behaviorism views our brain as a black box, and only outside stimuli can be controlled or manipulated, and it focuses on the premise that learning is an observed behavior (Saettler 1990). Learners rely on the instructor to model the behavior and learn through feedback from the instructor. The learner adjusts their behaviors to meet the criteria of the desired behavior based on instructor feedback. Instructors provide feedback through positive and negative reinforcement and positive and negative punishment. However, positive reinforcement has been shown to impact the learner most (Saettler 1990). Examples of positive reinforcement are instructors praising learners for correct answers, good grades, and rewards. It is believed that these types of reinforcement are prevalent within the K-12 classroom but fade away in higher education. One of the criticisms of behaviorism is that it ignores the higher level of thinking.

Cognitivism

Robert Gagne, David H. Jonassen, Richard Atkinson, Richard Shiffrin, and George A. Miller.

Cognitivism can be seen as a response to behaviorism (Ertmer & Newby, 1993), in that it fills in the gaps, so to speak, in behaviorism’s “black box” theory. Cognitivism has its roots in cognitive psychology (namely, Sigmund Freud), and it was also influenced by Gagne’s Theory of Instruction. The primary tenet of cognitivism is that learning occurs through changes in states of knowledge, rather than behavior (Ertmer & Newby, 1993). Shifrin’s Cognitive Information Processing Theory (CIP) helps to conceptualize and break down stages of processing information in the mind. Per the CIP, novel sensory input is received (attenuation), then passes from sensory memory to short-term or working memory. It is held temporarily in working memory by means of chunking, rehearsal, and pattern recognition, thus allowing it to be encoded into long-term memory. It can then be retrieved from long-term memory via retrieval cues. Cognitivism appears best suited for “complex forms of learning” (Ertmer & Newby, 1993, p. 59), such as reasoning and problem solving, where processing and retaining knowledge are emphasized. This differs from mastery of a physical skill or the adoption of an attitude or mindset.

Constructivism

Jean Piaget, Lev Vygotsky, Jerome Bruner, and Nelson Goodman.

Constructivists believe that the learner builds personal interpretations/knowledge of the world based on individual experiences and interactions, and that the learning occurs in situations within the context of our lives. In short, knowledge and the meaning of learning is subjective, created by the learner’s own mind- this is the key difference between constructivism and both behaviorism and cognitivism. Constructivism has been viewed as the dominant educational philosophy since the 1990s. It has become such prominence due to the following potential benefits. First, it corresponds to how people really learn. Second, it focuses on higher-order learning outcomes. Third, it integrates well with affect and emotion. Fourth, it is more relevant to job and out-of-the-classroom needs because of its emphasis on solving authentic problems (Wilson, 2018, p. 63). Constructivism could be a great way to integrate abstract knowledge with real-world problems, ​​especially for learning advanced knowledge in ill-structured domains.
